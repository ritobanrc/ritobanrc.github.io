Rating: 4/5

I've been meaning to write a review for this book for quite a while (I finished it near the end of last year), but only just got around to it about a month later, but hopefully I haven't forgotten too much. 

I really liked the premise of this book. You have a curmudgeony "get off my lawn"-type old fellow, he's super quirky, and initially the story starts out quite lighthearted, but it steadily grows more and more serious. This was particularly nice for me, since I started reading this book at the end of finals week last year, so it was nice to start with something quite light. 

The opening was absolutely hilarious -- I love the idea of Ove not knowing what an iPad is walking into the apple store and absolutely baffling the shit out of some poor employees. Indeed, that first chapter was what I read while at the library, and what convinced me to read this book. Extremely well done. 

## SPOILERS BELOW

The opening reveal, that Sonja is actually dead was also masterfully done -- the idea of someone acting as though their significant other is still alive, not able to accept that they've died is extremely powerful. You really get to see what makes Ove _tick_ -- that he has these very rigid patterns in his life which he follows near religiously.

From here, we launch into Ove's backstory (threaded through the main plotline. Of course, I'm a sucker for this style of backstory, love the Stormlight Archive), essentially all the ways that Ove has found meaning in life. At this point, it becomes essentially an existential text. Ove found meaning by following in the footsteps of his father, and then being loyal and working for the rail company, and then chasing after Sonja for a while, learning carpentry and construction as he built his house, staying loyal to Saab, creating the Resident's association in their neighborhood, enforcing its policies, etc.. I really liked this backstory--each of the major reveals was well executed (especially b)

The story opens after Sonja's death, and we find out that Ove is trying to commit suicide, but his plans are repeatedly foiled in rather comedic ways--someone backs their car onto his lawn, a cat (who is plainly supposed to be a representation of Sonja) stops him, he's unable to find certain parts, etc. The reveal that Ove is trying to commit suicide was also extremely well executed. I could feel myself tense up as I realized what he was about to do, and Backman really made us feel like we were inside Ove's head, that we could understand why Ove felt the way he did. 

Ultimately, the resolution of the book is that Ove finds meaning in supporting his new community, making peace with the fact that he's lost Sonja--his new neighbors, Parveneh and Patrick, Abel and Misrad, Jimmy the Clown, Anita and her husband (whose name escapes me right now). I  really like this message. I also strongly believe in the idea of finding one's meaning for oneself, and I think community and helping others is a really good one. It also explores the theme of moving on and coping with loss, and does a good job of that. 

My main criticism, however, is that the way this conclusion is presented feels quite rushed--we get the vast majority of the book in a slow and quite relaxed burn, but near the end, it feels like Backman realized that he needed to introduce a whole bunch of new characters to make his message of finding meaning in his community work. So, in an extremely short amount of time, we are introduced to Abel (a kid who works at a coffee shop and gets Ove's help to fix his bike, who Ove takes a liking to because he was one of Sonja's previous students),  Misrad (Abel's boss, also gay and closeted, has a homophobic father (Ove calling him "bent" was funny)), Jimmy (a clown who I think was also fat?), Patrick (Parveneh's husband, who I know nothing about), Anita and her husband (Ove's other neighbors, been friends with Ove for a long time), and probably a couple more I'm forgetting. And the trouble is, by introducing so many characters in such a short timespan, Backman really does not have time to develop most of them. I'm not invested in any of these other characters, they're just a jumble of names thrown at me to make it work. 

## END SPOILERS

Nonetheless, I thoroughly enjoyed this book. The prose was clean and crisp throughout, the pacing was generally well done (except at the conclusion), I often found myself laughing, but also contemplating serious themes, and Backman manages to do so very respectfully. Overall, I strongly recommend, with a rating of probably around a 4/5.
