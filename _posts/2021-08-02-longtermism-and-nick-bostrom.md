y'know -- it occurs to me that longtermism shares something with a rather disturbing brand of marxism
if you look back through the work of stalinist historians in the 1900s, who were stuck justifying the atrocities of stalin, mao. pol pot, guzman, and the rest, the common thread is "well eventually we might have had communism"
and it feels like longtermism is doing a similar thing here -- its ok if a million people die in an induced famine, because "eventually we might fulfill humanity's potential
like you could transpose the arguments onto each other -- if you have even a 1% chance of achieving communism, that's morally equivalent to 6 million ukranians dying in the holodomor
and i think ultimately the two arguments sorta suffer from the same problem -- they make an assumption about what course humanity will take, assume that achieving the end goal is the only thing that matters, and then single-mindedly pursue that goal
but just in general, i'm skeptical of end goals. one of the crucial tenets of anarchist thought is the idea that the means through which a society is built must reflect the characteristics society you desire -- if you want a society without a big oppressive government, you must not have a big oppressive government in the process of building that society, because there's no reason to imagine that the big oppressive government will vanish
if bostrom wants a society in which "human potential is fulfilled", if stalin wants a society in which "the state and class have been abolished, on the principle from each according to their ability, to each according to their need", they must lay out a pathway to the society that instills those same ideals and values. without that, we're just talking about meaningless platitudes and science fiction scenarios. and for bostrom, this is a problem, because his utopian vision of society simply cannot be one in which immediate human suffering is ignored for the dream of an even more distant utopia, but the method he prescribes for arriving at said utopia is exactly that. at what point do we stop ignoring immediate human suffering for the vision of a utopia, and suddenly realize that we are close enough to the utopia to begin to address the human suffering?


Of course, there are more immediate criticisms of Bostrom's work -- he often presents hypothetical, "even a 1% chance of saving $10^54$ people is worth it". But, obviously, the chance isn't 1%. If you gave me 10 minutes, I could trivially come up with more than 100 scenarios for the future of humanity, none of which result in $10^54$ people, and many of which are much worse. Bostrom has some math to justify the $10^54$ number, but he has absolutely no justification for the 1% number, and nor is it possible to have justification for it -- the probability of a particular ill-defined vaguely imagined scenario millions of years in the future is simply not something that is calculable, and that lets Bostrom pick a laughably high number like 1%. For all we know, that number is actually closer to $10^{-128}\%$, and Bostrom's philosophy completely falls apart. And _crucially_, Bostrom and I have exactly the same amount of evidence for $1\%$ and $10^{-128}\%$ -- if anything, I have slightly more, since there's good reason to be skeptical of the claim of 1%. At that point, Bostrom might as well have claimed that a magic 8-ball or a 2000-year-old book told him what to believe, since _literally_ all he's done is picked a random number out of thin air, and written a bunch of philosophy based on it.
