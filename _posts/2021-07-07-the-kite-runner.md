This book took me quite a bit longer to read than normal (143 days!), mostly cause school was back in full swing and I was really busy with classes and APs and extracurriculars and really didn't have much time for reading at all. I'm really hoping that next year I'll be able to read throughout the school year more regularly -- it kinda sucks when the bulk of your reading happens during the summer. 

The Kite Runner was an interesting book. I'd first heard about this book in the first quarter of this school year, because Samar had presented it for his Novel Focus Group book, and it sounded really interesting, so I decided to add it to my reading list, and I've gotten around to reading it. 

There was a lot that I really liked about this book. The premise was one of the most emotional that I've ever seen -- while I didn't cry at any point, the scene where Hassan was raped, and near the end, when Amir and Sohrab and running kites both had me close to tears. The emotional scenes scattered throughout -- Sohrab attempting to commit suicide, Hasan and Ali leaving, discovering Hassan is Amir's half-brother, returning to Kabul and seeing it so differed each time, Amir's father's death scene -- were all extremely high impact and quite well written. 

Altogether, though I did not notice this until after I was done, in hindsight, the characters are quite one-dimensional -- Amir is a bit egotistical, but guilt-ridden over his actions in the beginning, Hassan is unyieldingly loyal to Amir, Baba is stuck-up in his old ways, etc. So in that sense, I'd say the book is largely plot driven -- if I think about this book, I'm going to remember the major events (Hassan being raped, Amir and Baba leaving for America, marrying Soraya, returning to Afghanistan to meet Hassan, etc.). 

Earlier today, I discovered that the Kite Runner is considered _problematic_ by some people: https://www.reddit.com/r/books/comments/ofocct/i_just_finished_the_kite_runner_by_khaled/h4ern0d/. Particularly, the instagram story linked by the reddit comment does a good job deconstructing many of inaccuracies presented in Hosseini's presentation of Afghanistan. In a short list

* The Pashtun-Hazara conflict isn't nearly as one-sided as Hosseini portrayed it as. Pashtun's are not all Taliban, they are not all evil, and there are several other ethnic groups in Afghanistan (Uzbeks, Tajiks, etc.)
* Leaving out the US intervention (support for the Taliban under Soviet control, funding opium farming) an explanatory factor in Afghanistan's descent seems shady, and is particularly heinous when considering the Hosseini is friends with Bush, (may have) supported the American invasion in Afghanistan, and that this book was published right around the time the US government needed to drum up support for the war.
* More broadly, Hosseini engages in what can only be described as "tragedy porn" -- creating a sad, historically inaccurate story, then profiting off of it as western audiences feel sympathy for those who have been hurt.
* In his other books, Hosseini apparently regularly portrays Afghan women as helplessly oppressed by the Taliban, needing to be saved (by white people, it is implied), a common neo-conservative, and more generally, colonial talking point. 
    - colonialism uniquely utilizes a characterization of patriarchal structures as abhorrent among the colonized to justify its hierarchical structure being imposed -- which neither dismantles the original patriarchal structure (because the colonizer's culture also features a similar patriarchy), nor liberates the colonized economically (because the colonial structure is imposed upon the colonized)

Altogether, the high-impact emotional scenes combined with the tight, well built narrative make this an enjoyable read. Hosseini's command of language is deft, if not as masterful as Steinbeck or Tolkein, making it a pleasure to read. Would strongly recommend to other people, though with a caution about Hosseini's characterization of Afghanistan and the politics that it might imply.
