title  : Phantom of the Opera
tags: [book-review]
---

author : Gaston Lereux
start  :  1 Jun 2020
end    :  7 Jun 2020
rating : 3.5

I honestly don't know what I was expected going into this. I decided to read this after seeing Lindsey Ellis discussing it so many times, and one excerpt she read, involving the lines "You asinine Persian dolt" and "Fuck your customs" struck me as intriguing. I assume I must have concluded from those lines that this was some sort of murder mystery/thriller/drama type story. I was dismayed to find out that not only are those lines not in it, but the plot was nothing like I expected. It is a tragic romance!

I quite enjoyed the beginning and the introduction. The prose flowed really beautifully, carrying me into the story and gently placing me down. The mystery of the Opera Ghost seemed to be the central plot point, and it was somewhat obvious from the start that the Opera Ghost was the same as Christine's Angel of Music. The scenes involving the new managers were brilliantly executed.

That is unfortunately more than I can say about the romance. The romance felt manufactured and artificial, and while I can see that Christine is an allusion to Sirens, drawing in Raoul, and I simply thought most of his actions were creepy and possessive. Unlike God of Small Things, where I felt that there was a lot of symbolism that I was missing out on, here, I felt like that symbolism just didn't land in the first place. I'm still not sure why the road is the Communists Road, or how exactly the hexagonal torture mirror room worked.

The ending was kind of bizarre. I think I'm supposed to feel empathetic toward the ghost -- Raoul is an ass, after all, and Lereux does end by talking about how Erik is what society made him out to be (though I didn't really catch on to this until Lindsey Ellis pointed it out to me). As she pointed out, this wasn't a stellar book, and that's not what it set out to be. I don't know why I expected it to become my new favorite book (that was mostly a reaction to some of the truly extraordinary prose at the beginning), but it was fine romantic genre fiction.
