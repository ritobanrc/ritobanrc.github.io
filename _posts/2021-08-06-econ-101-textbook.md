i'm looking at Paul Krugman's econ 101 textbook
and it starts off with a series of assumptions, much the same as a physicist would assume air resistance and friction are zero
except these assumptions are a bit peculiar
1. Resources are scarce
2. The true cost is the opportunity cost
3. Decisions tend to be marginal decisions
4. People exploit opportunities to make themselves "better off"
5. There are gains from trade (comparative advantage)
6. Markets move towards equillibrium
7. Resources should be used as efficiently as possible to achieve society's goals
8. Markets lead to efficiency
Q. E. fucking. D
in fifteen pages, Krugman seems to have categorically decided that the free market system is invariably the best
never mind that literally the next section talks about how markets aren't always perfect, and essentially the rest of economics as a discipline is slowly but surely trying to quantify exactly why markets fail in each scenario where they don't work
the assumptions that economics started with almost tautologically justify free markets -- so no matter how much work you do to point out "hey markets aren't actually all that efficient at dstributing. or healthcare. or scientific research. or infrastructure. or the commons (look up elinor ostrom's nobel prize winning work)." -- the overall consensus will almost always remain "free markets good"

DuBois), then talking a bit about each of their theoretical perspectives (structural functionalism of Herbert Spencer, conflict theory for Karl Marx, symbolic interactionism of for Mead), then it talks about how empirical sociological research is conducted, and finally, it gets to actual content, which starts by trying to define "Culture" and then different types of "Social Interaction" -- that's a fundamentally different approach, instead of starting with assumptions about interactions within a market setting, it seeks to define social interaction in general.
