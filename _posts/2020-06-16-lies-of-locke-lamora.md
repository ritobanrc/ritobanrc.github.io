author : Scott Lynch
start  : 08 May 2020
end    : 25 May 2020 
rating : 4.5

_The Lies of Locke Lamora_ is a wonderful fantasy novel. I was particularly intrigued by the cover art. It's set in some Italian city, ruled simultaneously by a nobility and several gangs of thieves, all ruled over by a Capa Barsavi.

- Prose
    - The prose isn't flowery by any measure, but even maintaining its laconic style, he manages to _very_ effectively set the mood of scenes. You can really feel how the entire world is being affected and is effecting the plot and our main characters. This also allowed some really nice development of metaphors and allusions. Locke going down to hell (meeting with Capa Barsavi + Celebration afterward), like Perseus, coming back up, and then climbing to Olympus, where the "Gods" aren't so different, and are still partying, or the lovely descriptions of Falselight or Elderglass. Some of the best laconic, terse prose I've seen.
- Magic System
    - The story features 2 sorts of magic systems (roughly). There's alchemy, which is implied to be science based and is largely used for ordinary, mundane tasks like lighting. It really reminds me of fabrials from Stormlight, and is quite well done. The other magic system is the bondsmagi, who are portrayed as mysterious, part of an order that will hunt you down if you cross them. Much of the latter half of the book is concerned with Locke dealing with one of these. I think it could be greatly benefited from this being a hard-magic system, because we don't really know the capabilities of the bondsmagi. For all we know, they could kill the main characters at a distance with a thought, but we never see them do this. The whole "true name" thing also struck me as somewhat odd -- not only does it seem to be an extremely low bar to clear, but there doesn't seem to be any limit on distance, and I didn't quite figure out how it fit with the overall themes of the story.
- Themes
    - It is rare to leftist or socialist themes in fantasy, but Lynch does this very well. He very succinctly explains how the dukes exploit and steal from the poor, and does a brilliant job showing how similar they are to the actual thieves. Indeed, the entire premise of the "Gentlemen Bastards" is that the so-called "Gentlemen" are more bastardly than they appear at first, and are able to blend in so well with the aristocracy, because they are doing the same thing -- playing political games and trying to steal from others. 
    - What's very uniquely impressive though, is that this story still features Locke _saving_ the nobles. Raza is bad, not because he opposes the nobles, but because he's a vindictive asshole! Which is a really great way to approach this -- hate the system, not the players.
- Pacing
    - Really well done. For the first half or so, each chapter is adding one more complication to Locke's already tenuous situation, and the tension builds up really well, so you can immediately feel how satisfying the resolution is going to be. The only criticism I have is that around 70% through the book, all the tension is almost resolved -- Locke has been saved by Jean, Calo, Galdo and Bug are dead, Capa Barsavi rules. This releases a lot of the tension, I think maybe introducing the Satisfaction a bit earlier, or adding in some complications with the Salvaras earlier might have smoothed this out a little bit, but overall, the pacing is really good.
- Locke
    - This reminds me a lot of Kingkiller -- Locke is a bit of a Mary Sue, much like Kvothe. Even the one thing he can't do, fight, where he has to rely on Jean, kinda gets ignored -- he beats Raza in a fight, and I just don't accept that him bluffing in the middle of a fight is similar enough to his skills of ungodly planning. Regardless, Locke is still an extremely well realized character, I felt genuinely sad at Calo, Galdo, and Bug's deaths. Really well written overall, just a bit more consistency would have been appreciated.
- Humor/Dramatic Irony
    - I don't really expect there to be humor in fantasy, beyond a couple of dumb jokes, but Lynch pulls it off really well. Sometime, you're just left laughing at the sheer audacity of the plans that Locke pulls of, swindling literally everybody. Lynch is really the master of dramatic irony.
- Raza
    - I'm not a fan of villain backstories, they're very, very rarely done well. One does not simply become a villain because of one event sometime in your life, but it seems to us that Raza wants to commit just to get revenge on one particular event. I would have liked to see him be a bit more nuanced, or at least shown to have had an extremely disturbing childhood. One event feels a bit lackluster.
