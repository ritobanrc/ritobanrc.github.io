title: Effect of Minimum Wage on Inflation
date: 2022-01-20
---

Overall, the effect negative effect of minimum wage on inflation is not actually that much, and sometimes counteracted with other policy: 

* http://ftp.iza.org/dp1072.pdf (a 2004 meta-analysis of dozens of studies, estimating the effect of a 10% increase in minimum wage is a 0.4% increase in overall prices (though a 4% increase in food prices)
* https://doi.org/10.1016/j.econlet.2020.109051 (this one from mexico notes that inflation decreased by an average of 1.8% following a doubling of the minimum wage and cutting VAT taxes (which are regressive), with minimum wage likely contributing little to none of that), 
*  https://www.jstor.org/stable/41445397  (this one from vietnam concludes several minimum wage increases in the period from 1994-2008 did not increase inflation in the long term),
*  https://doi.org/10.1080/00036840410001674213 (this one from france from 1970 to '94, concludes that minimum wage increases may have initially caused inflation, in the long term, the effect is negligible or disinflationary)

 there's a couple of reasons for this, the obvious one being that inflation is not solely a product of the money that the poorest have, raising the minimum wage doesn't push everyone else's wages up as well, so the overall inflation won't be affected as much (though that is probably why food prices are disproportionately affected, since poorer folk spend a larger proportion of their income on food). additionally,  [this article](https://doi.org/10.2307/2235571) notes that  minimum wage isn't like printing money or decreasing interest rates, it doesn't make money appear out of nowhere, it merely redistributes money from employers/consumers to (poor) employees. in particular, minimum wage employees in service jobs are disproportionately bought by high-income consumers. the paper concludes increasing the minimum wage is a "modestly effective redistributive tool". 

the impact of minimum wage on employment levels is widely accepted to be overblown btw: https://www.reddit.com/r/Economics/wiki/faq_minwage

A more meaningful question, however, than the effect of minimum wage on inflation is the effect of minimum wage on quality of life, and secondly impact on the overall economy. [This](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ijhm.2019.04.019) Malaysian article suggests increasing minimum wage increases compensation satisfaction and work motivation, which in turn increases job satisfaction and perceived quality of life, and that work motivation significantly increased work engagement. The methodology of the article is a little bit strange, but it definitely does suggest increasing the minimum wage (a) increases quality of life and (b) increases how much people want/are willing to work. [This article](https://doi.org/10.1111/ijsw.1211) from Hong Kong's methodology is less strange, and concludes that there is a dramatic positive effect on quality of life and job satisfaction, and makes some pretty sweeping conclusions about the positive effect of a minimum wage. 

Overall, I'm honestly surprised by how uniformly pro-minimum wage the _empirical_ evidence is -- my understanding was that though the tides were slowly shifting, economists tended to be opposed to minimum wages because the free-market models that much of economics is based on refute floors or ceilings on the prices of goods. And while there certainly is still some literature opposing the minimum wage, a lot of it appeared to be largely theoretical or driven by mathematical modelling (or just old). Empirical studies on the effect of minimum wage appear to only have been seriously undertaken starting in the 1990s, and it seems like the empirical works uniformly find the negative impacts to be small or nonexistent, and the positive impacts quite significant.
