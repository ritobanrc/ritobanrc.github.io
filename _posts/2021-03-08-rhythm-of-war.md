Well, it's been a while since I read a Stormlight book. It's rather unfortunate that I didn't enjoy this one nearly as much as I enjoyed some of the other ones. This one felt solidly mediocre -- the plot was predictable at nearly every step of the way, character development felt lacking, only existing in service of the plot, and the prose felt worse than usual. That really is a shame, because I _really_ wanted to like this book. The artwork is beautiful, above and beyond anything in the previous books. The themes of mental health are far more fully developed than in any of the previous books. 

## Story
There are 3 different plotlines in this book. 

* First, there's the Urithiru plot line, which involves Navani, Kaladin, and to a lesser extent, Venli.
    - This plotline was by far the most fully developed of the three, and it was the only one that was any good. Navani's conflict with Raboniel was, if not engrossing, at least somewhat engaging. I appreciated the development we got in the magic system -- the pure tones of Roshar, the Rhythm of War, the anti-Voidlight. I think this was the plotline where I was _supposed_ to start identifying with the Listeners a bit more, and while I definitely somewhat like Venli, and can intellectually recognize that the two groups working together is important, Sanderson needs to do a lot more work to get me to emotionally identify with them. The fact that a number of the fused leaders (namely Leshwi and Raboniel) support reconciliation, rather than outright victory is a really interesting conceit, and I'd really like to see it further developed. But besides that, this plotline really got a bit repetitive -- it was just Navani and Raboniel politicking against each other, and Navani occasionally sending Kaladin out on an errand. Even the resolution was unsatisfying, it almost felt like Navani forced the Sibling to bond her. I think the Sibling bonding Rlain or Dabbid would have been a much better symbol and could have served as a much better jumping off point for approaching the reconciliation in future books. 
    - Kaladin's involvement in this plotline was largely unsatisfying. This isn't to say I expected him to resolve his mental health issues -- though I know some people have said they liked that. I do recognize that mental health issues take a long time to sort out, there's ups and downs -- I would have liked to see more of that portrayed.  We start out with him getting his family from Hearthstone, which, despite being under Singer rule, seems to be doing fine. Kaladin somehow manages to convince the entire city to come with him (I'm still shocked that his parents didn't object more to this, but I digress). Upon arriving in Urithiru, there's sorta a subplot with Kaladin's father not wanting him to be a soldier, and it appears that they have arrived at a resolution with Kaladin starting him mental health facility. As is usual with Sanderson, the symbolism is a bit blunt, but I don't really have a big issue with that. Instead, is more that, after going to all of the effort to build up Kaladin's therapy group, we basically get no payoff. For the entire rest of the book, Kaladin is little more than a mercenary, fighting when necessary, struggling with his powers at other times. I would have liked to see more time focussed on his mental health struggle. Maybe that's just me, because I _really_ don't care for portrayals of fighting in fantasy in general, but in general, I felt like Kaladin spend most of his time fighting, and would have liked to see him do other stuff, really anything.
    - This isn't to say there weren't moments in this plotline I didn't like. The discovery of the Rhythm of War was great, Raboniel's reveal about her daughter was great, Moash's return was great, Lift was great. But as a whole, it could have been a lot better.
* Second, there's the Shadesmar plotline, involving Shallan and Adolin's trial
    - This plotline was probably the most predictable thing I have ever read. The whodunnit plotline with the Mriaze cube went on for wayyy too long, and was super obvious from the start. The trial scene with Adolin was cringey as fuck. Even barring the fact that Kalak talks like a surfer dude or something, which I might have been fine with, everything about the trial is just awful. From the way its structured, to the lack of _any_ arguments, its just cringey. Couldn't he get like a lawyer to look over it??? Like obviously it doesn't have to mimic the US legal system, but it should at least mimic _a_ legal system. Even Tyrion's trial in game of thrones was a reasonably accurate portrayal of medieval trials. Adolin's was just awful. And that's not even getting into all the structural issues with this. Just to run down them quickly:
    - It was obvious from the beginning that Maya would revive/talk/something at the end of the trial. The reveal that the spren chose the Recreance was interesting, but that's it. 
    - The whole thing at the end where the other honorspren tries to replace Kalak as the judge cause Shallan was talking to him was utterly pointless, had no bearing on the plot whatsoever.
    - Shallan's _I wonder who Restares is_ was contrived and pointless, iirc, the whole "ooh I've fallen and injured myself please give me stormlight" was stupid. 
    - Shallan's Formless reveal, though interesting, was underdeveloped. She was Formless for like 1 chapter, and then everything was back to normal. 
    - I'm really sad about this, because there was so much promise in this plotline. We could have learned more about honorspren and their customs, we could have seen Adolin grow as a character, we could have seen Shallan really deal with her DID, but we kinda just... didn't.
* Finally, there's Dalinar and Jasnah's plotline off in Iri
    - We barely spent any time here. A small handful of things happen. (a) Jasnah likes democracy! Yay! (b) Wit says stuff??   (c) There are a couple of cool battle scenes.  That's about it. 
    - Except one big reveal. Taravangian is now Odium!!! That's huge!! That's an amazing reveal, it's pretty much the one reason why RoW gets a 5.5 instead of something less than a 5. 

## Character
* Basically, I would have liked to see more of everything. In every book so far, we've basically done a character study of 1 character. This was supposed to be Eshonai/Venli's book -- it wasn't, I don't think those flashbacks revealed a single thing to us. I suppose that Kalak was the one who told Venli about Szeth was interesting, but that's about it. We didn't get anything out of the Listener tribes or whatever they were, we didn't get anything new out of seeing Venli's first interactions with Ulim, besides ig she was jealous of Estonia?
* This book could have been Navani's character study -- really looking into her childhood, seeing how her marriage with Gavilar developed. 
* Besides that, I didn't see much development on Shallan and Adolin, and I already discussed some of my problems with Kaladin's development. 
    - I _liked_ Shallan's DID struggle -- it would have been really interesting to read more about. But the resolution was so abrupt and so rapid, but also so obvious in the making, that it just wasn't interesting to read about.

## Pacing/Prose
I've added "Prose" as a category here (it used to be just Pacing), solely for this book, because Sanderson's prose here was _sooo bad_. Like normally, his prose is one of his weaker aspects, but it's not awful. In RoW, it was very much awful -- and amplified because the plot was dull in a lot of places. Good prose can save a bad plot (Wuthering Heights LMAO), and good plot can save bad prose, but here, both fell kidna flat.

I know that Sanderson is capable of writing good prose, I've seen him do it. It just... wasn't here. Every description was straightforward, every explanation hammed into you like a textbook. Every so often, I'd encounter a line that was actually well written, and I'd highlight it -- usually, they had some beautiful imagery or something. 

Combined with this was a bunch of anachronistic neologism. Obviously, Kalak (though that was clearly very concious). Less concious, I imagine, was using the words "cringeworthy" and "awesome", which make it feel like I'm listening to a twelve year old, or the fact that Navani refers to "electrocuting", even though electricity hadn't been invented. Even the word "emulsifier", which features prominently, feels like a neologism. After all, the world emulsifier only came into existence with mass-scale industrial food engineering, where chemical emulsifiers were needed to make massive quantities of emulsions -- before then, for centuries, chefs just called them "the stuff you used to thicken sauce, like protein or flour". Admittedly, the online etymology dictionary says the word emulsion has been around since 1610, but it still feels too "industrial" to use here. 

## World
When I wrote this for Oathbringer, I said "it's fucking Roshar" and gave it a 10/10. Here, I've decided to focus more in on how well the setting was integrated in service of the plot, and how well integrated it was. Really, there's only two in question: Urithiru and Lasting Integrity.

* Urithiru was kinda interesting, ig? I would have liked to see more use of all of the fancy fabrials and other tech and stuff in the tower, beyond that, it's just a fancy castle with some secret passageways for Kaladin and Lift to prowl around in, but ig that's kinda cool?
* The only interesting bit of Lasting Integrity is the "gravity changes direction", which is an interesting conceit, but extent to which its used is the aforementioned Shallan accidentally injuring herself scene, which is stupid. This had _so much potential_, and I'm honestly a bit disappointed he didn't do more here.

## Themes
Finally, themes. Sanderson does a decent job here. The two big ones that he carries through are mental health and lingering effects of colonization and enslavement. Mental health is great, not much to say that already hasn't been said, I would have liked to see more of Kaladin, and a lot of it is really blunt.
