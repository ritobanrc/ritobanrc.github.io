This was an interesting book. The premise is quite interesting, examining the mental state of someone after they've committed murder. (I read the Garnett translation).

The primary reason I've rated this so low is because much of the book is extremely slow and often hard to read. For a while, a wrote this off as intentional, Dostoevsky examining the fraught and disorganized nature of Raskolnikov's descent into insanity, because often, the other characters perspectives were better. But I came to realize that there was quite a significant slog that I just wasn't enjoying. I simply couldn't bring myself to care about the whole Sonia plotline, and regularly lost track of all the Ivanonovas. Perhaps part of this could have been solved if I had a more modern translation (I was reading the one by Constance Garnett)

However, this is quite a bit that I did like:

- Razumihin has got to be one of the best friendships I've read in literature. The sheer amount that he cares for Raskolnikov is amazing.
- The ending. The last 15% of the book was really well done. Svidrigailov's crimes being revealed, him pretty much kidnapping Dounia, Dounia being amazing and escaping

I really didn't pick up on a lot of the symbolism that I'm sure exists -- like what Marmeledovs story, etc.

# Themes/Politics

This section is going to be entirely bullshit, don't put any stock in it, but here are just some random ideas I had.

While reading this, I had the misfortune of stumbling across a JP video discussing Dostoevsky. Like everything else he says, JP's interpretation is impressively bullshit. He reads this as a repudiation of utilitarianism and socialism. Amusingly, he talks about how Dostoevsky avoids strawmanning utilitarianism by making its proponents some of the more reasonable people in the story. What he doesn't realize is that his own argument is literally "Utilitarianism is when you murder random people for the sake of it".  

I don't think Dostoevsky that clearly presents a refutation of the idea that perhaps some crime is reasonable for "the greater good". Indeed, even at the end, Raskolnikov, despite accepting his punishment, maintains that the murder was justified. But at the same time, Dostoevsky does a decent job arguing for same base moral values, opposing concepts of egoism and rationalism -- in a sense, he refutes Randian rational egoism before it exists! 

You can see the beginnings of the concept of the Ubermanch come to fruition, and I'm reminded of a legal eagle video I watched, which describes a case where 2 college students murder a little boy simply for the sake of understanding what it would feel like to commit murder, because they believed themselves to be "supermen".
