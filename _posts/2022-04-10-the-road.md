Rating: 6.5 

_The Road_, by Cormac McCarthy, is a really interesting work of post-apocalyptic dystopian fiction that follows the desperate journey of a man and his child as they try to reach the coast, overcoming hunger, starvation, bandits, cannibals, and much more. 

Ultimately, the reason the book doesn't get a higher rating is because of pacing -- I felt as though there were distinct sections where the pacing felt quite slow, and I had to force myself to keep reading. Nonetheless, the book is absolutely worth it. McCarthy has an incredible ability to make you _care_ about characters, even when they find themselves in situations where he throws every imaginable hurdle at them, and then some. I often found myself tearing up at a small comment the young child made, where you could really see that he was internalizing the bleakness of their situation, and was barely holding on to hope.

I really enjoyed the setting of the book--we can tell that its a post-apocalyptic future set in the United States, where civilization has collapsed and nearly all life as ceased to exist. I was curious whether McCarthy would tell us any details about what kind of disaster befell the civilization in the book--I think the most likely situation is a nuclear war and the ensuing fallout from radiation, but it could potentially be an extreme climate change scenario. McCarthy doesn't actually tell us, and I'm happy with that choice--I think leaving it ambiguous works better, as it forces us to reckon with the many possibilities it could be, and confront the fact that we may be closer to that world than we'd like to believe.

I found the end of the story quite interesting--in some sense, the story is about how the young child is the one speck of home in the man's life, and his wide-eyed innocence and desire to comprehend the distraught world around him let the reader empathize and feel for the young child. On the other hand, the story also shows how the man and his child themselves _cannot_ always act as ethically as they want to, and I think the conclusion brilliantly underscores this idea--sometimes they _must_ act cruel in order to survive.

McCarthy's style here is also quite unique--the story is largely told through colloquies between the man and the boy (who remain unnamed throughout the story), interspersed with descriptions from a narrator, and occasional flashbacks into the man's past. The colloquies really allowed me to immerse myself into the story, the lack of quotation marks made it feel like I was just hearing the talking (as opposed to hearing someone recount the talking), and altogether fostered an extremely intimate relationship between me and the characters.

Overall, the emotional range McCarthy takes you through in _The Road_ is virtually unsurpassed. I've rarely felt such elation as I did when the man and the boy found the stores of food, or the terror when they had their possessions stolen, or the horror at realizing the extent to which other people resorted to cannibalism. _The Road_ is an incredible book, and barring some small pacing problems, where I felt the book was dragging on with little progress, I would strongly recommend people read it at least once.
