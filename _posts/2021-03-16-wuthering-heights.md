* Author : Emily Bronte
* Start  : 27 Jul 2020
* End    : 12 Aug 2020
* Rating : 3.8

This was a really interesting book, and I don't really know how to analyze it, or even rate or react to it. I chose to read this out of one of the choice readings for Murray's (now nonexistent) AP Lit summer homework (it became AP Lang). I don't really know what I was expected, I know that Sophia the senior? who Emily was friends with talked about it a lot. There's some really interesting analyses in the introduction of my edition, which I'll have to read through in detail at some point.

Anyway, the book started with some really, just _amazingly_ lovely prose. Every single sentence is just amazingly crafted:

> Before passing the threshold, I paused to admire a quantity of grotesque carving lavished over the front, and especially about the principal door, above which, among a wilderness of crumbling griffins, and shameless little boys, I detected the date '1500', and the name 'Hareton Earnshaw'.

Unfortunately, as the novel went on, I began to find this prose more tedious than flowery, especially when slang that I was not familiar with, or North English accents, got involved.



I can't really talk much about the themes. I'd say that from a cursory look, Heathcliff definitely seemed to be something of an ass. Like calling Isabela a slut, shaming her, being vindictive again Hindley, abusing Catherine (younger), kidnapping her and forcing her into marriage. All of it is really straight up despicable. And yet, he's somehow still vaguely sympathetic. This book really _masters_ morally grey characters.

I also find it an interesting choice to tell the story entirely from the perspective of servants -- Nelly, and at one point, Zillah. It's fascinating that Nelly, despite being our only source of information, is revealed to be an unreliable narrator, always placing her own master, Edgar Linton in a positive light, using words like "ruffian" to describe Hareton and Heathcliff.

Some of the scenes weren't as flagrantly emotional as perhaps WoK, but they were really subtle, and the emotions I felt were really complex. Honestly, even though I might not have enjoyed reading this all the time, simply cause of how long it dragged on, I can definitely see why this is a classic. The way the language is crafted, the themes, the symbolism, is really masterful.
