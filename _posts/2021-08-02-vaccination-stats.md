but a couple days ago, a whole bunch of major news sources started making claims of "vaccinated people transmit the delta variant at the same rate as unvaccinated people" cause the CDC made a presentation that if you interpreted it incorrectly, you could arrive at that conclusion

so the study was done Provincetown, Mass., where nearly the entire population is vaccinated -- so the fact that 3/4 cases were in vaccinated people isn't all that surprising, since virtually everyone is vaccinated

yeah, so that's the next thing, the study concludes that _in breakthrough infections_ viral load between vaccinated and unvaccinated people is similar for the delta variant -- but breakthrough infections are rare, and that's the piece that most news agencies missed in their headlines

https://www.reuters.com/business/healthcare-pharmaceuticals/delta-infections-among-vaccinated-likely-contagious-lambda-variant-shows-vaccine-2021-08-02/

https://www.medrxiv.org/content/10.1101/2021.07.28.21261295v1 oh another thing that this study (first one linked in the reuters article) points out in the abstract is that viral load _decreases_ significantly faster in vaccinated individuals (even for the delta variant)

so while the provincetown data shows that viral load might get up to the same levels in vaccinated people with the delta variant, it will also decrease significantly faster, meaning (a) they will transmit it for less time, and (b) they will have less serious cases (which the study notes as well, vaccinated individuals were considerably older, needed less oxygen, etc)

> The mRNA vaccines are highly effective at preventing symptomatic and severe COVID-19 associated with B.1.617.2 [Delta] infection. Vaccination is associated with faster decline in viral RNA load and a robust serological response.

that's the study's conclusion -- the vaccine prevents symptomatic and _severe_ disease
