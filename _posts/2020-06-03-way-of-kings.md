The first time I read this, I really enjoyed this. While the beginning was slow, once I realized how the different plot lines were coming together, it was amazing.

# Reread #1
However, I just reread it, and I'm significantly more disappointed than last time.

Things I liked:
- I didn't realize how early some of the foreshadowing was
    - Shallan seeing the patterns in her sketches
    - Kaladin seeing Syl starting very early
    - Moash being a power-hungry vindictive asshole from the beginning
- The battle of the tower was amazing once again. I teared up when Kaladin went back for Dalinar.
- Shallan -- a lot of people dislike Shallan for being the "YA girl", caucasian, overly attractive (but doesn't realize it), witty about literally everything, has guy(s) fawning over her, "not like other girls". But I think Shallan actually subverts, even satirises this trope, which I liked a lot
    - Her wit and humor is crass, turning it into a tension point between her and Jasnah. It regularly gets her into awkward situations
    - The guy fawning over her is actually trying to murder Jasnah
    - She thinks she's super smart, but is really effectively humbled by Jasnah
    - The stereotypical, moody, melodramatic teenager's suicide attempt is replaced by soulcasting the goblet, which is a brilliant subversion
- I enjoyed seeing Elhokar's feasts, it's great seeing the sheer opulence.

Things I did not like:
- All of Kal's backstory
    - Normally, in a backstory, you expect there to be some hidden details, some subtleties that you missed earlier, some foreshadowing. There was _nothing_. Literally nothing interesting beyond the core plot happened. You know from the beginning that Kal is going to decide to be a surgeon, because, obviously, Sanderson's not going to be your protagonist a bloodthirsty maniac. And once you know the ending (that Roshone sends Tien into the army, Kal follows), there's literally nothing of substance here. I might have enjoyed it if Sanderson had some beautiful prose, but his prose is laconic, to the point where it's the bare minimum to visualize whats going on and nothing more. 
- We needed more time with Depressed Kaladin. We only get to _see_ one bridge run before he decides to save everyone. And while it is implied that there have been several bridge runs, without page-time, it just doesn't have the same emotional impact. Maybe I'm pulling too much from Wheel of Time, where the plot creeps along at a snails pace (in a good way), but this genuinely felt like Brandon was holding a yoke behind the plot forcing it along.
- Here's the big one: betrayal of theme
    - For most of Kaladin's arc, we have some really anarchist themes. The Alethi caste system is obviously horrible, and Bridge Four sorta presents a way to transcend that -- through camaraderie and mutual aid. There's even a point where Moash is like "I wanna be a lighteyes" and everyone else says "you'd become just as oppressive as them." and Moash says "i'd be different! I'd make Sadeas pay, he'd be the one running bridges" or something to that effect, and the bridge crew is like "See, you've creative another oppressive hiearchy. that's bad". There's also some really great themes of mutual aid that are brought up repeatedly -- if everyone chips in a little bit, we can get this to save so-and-so, or whatever. This honestly felt like something written by Kroptkin or Bakunin or Weber at times. And I was really enjoying this, because fantasy generally features monarchies, with one person having the "right" to be monarch because they have better genes than everyone else (quite literally, in the case of Aragorn). Usually, I'm willing to forgive that, because the monarchy is little more than a tool to develop the plot and the themes further. Unfortunately, in the case of Stormlight...
    - This all changes when Kaladin meets Dalinar. I honestly don't mind Kaladin having to find the "one good lighteyes". I don't buy into the "we gotta kill all of them" dogma that some socialists/anarchists buy into. Frederick Engels was a factory owner. BUT, my problem is that the second Kaladin joins Dalinar, he COMPLETELY forgets that huge swaths of the population are still living under this horrific caste system. It honestly feels like, "look there's one good cop, so Not ACAB!". Except all Lighteyes are Bastards.
    - It's kinda like writing a story about how a slave needs to "get over" his hatred for the slave master. Cause... he doesn't. He needs to get _rid_ of the slavemaster. But Dalinar just doesn't try to do this. It honestly feels like Brandon is just saying, "well, times up! the main character is in a position of power now, and I have other shit to do! PLOT EXIGENCY".
- Brandon's prose. It just isn't good. Everything is so terse, so laconic, that it just doesn't evoke emotion. Good, vivid prose can keep me interested even if the story isn't interesting. But Brandon just doesn't do that. 

Overall, I was pretty bored out of my mind until the end. I'm putting this squarely at the same place as Crossroads of Twilight, the worst WoT book according to most of the community.
