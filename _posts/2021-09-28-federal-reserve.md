in the american system of government, congress passes budget legislation, describing how the president should spend money, then the president implements that legislation using the budget. separate from that, congress also passes taxes, which is totally unrelated to the executive branch's budget 

now unsurprisingly, congress does not like to raise taxes very much, so the president is stuck spending more money than he has 

now the solution to this is to borrow money -- in particular, the president borrows money from the american public (read: rich people) in the form of bonds, which are like stocks, but for the american government, and then pays back those bonds over an extremely long period of time  (usually 10 years), with interest (these are called the 10 year treasury notes, though there are other kinds as well, ranging from 2 years to 30 years, collectively called "treasury securities"). 

the amount that the US government owes in bonds is called the debt -- the US continues to make regular debt payments (paying out a portion of each bond) every 6 months. However, congress has also passed a debt ceiling -- the maximum legal amount that the president is allowed to borrow. Now, y'see, the US debt keeps going up. Normally, that's not a problem, we've never missed a debt payment, so investors know that they'll eventually get their money, and continue to buy the 10 year treasury notes -- generally, its described as "the most stable investment in the world" 

but, every couple of years, the debt gets really really close to that ceiling, and there's a big hullabaloo about it, and congress eventually lifts it

however, if theoretically, congress didn't lift it, what would happen is the US government would run out of money, and would not be able to make its payments to its investors (called a default, we would default on our debt, meaning we just... wouldn't pay it). further, the government would not be able to pay its employees (cause its run out of money), which is what's called a government shutdown -- now that's also something of a political tactic, if you're the minority party in congress, and you manage to block a bill to raise the debt ceiling (or fail to pass a budget bill, which is another way to cause a shutdown), then you can be like "oh look biden is so incompetent he cause the government to shutdown" and make a big deal about it

realistically, mcconnell has said he "Will support a clean continuing resolution that will prevent a government shutdown" -- so its not gonna happen, the government almost certainly isn't gonna shut down, and even if it doesn, even if we do default on our debt, congress will probably pretty quickly pass something that goes back and pays the debt we defaulted on -- the disaster scenarios that some economists like to imagine (this is going to plunge the country into a recession as investors lose faith in the US government and the dollar) are kinda ridiculous

but theoretically, here's the justification for why defaulting on the US debt could cause a recession. US dollars (post-1931) are what's called "fiat currency", which means there isn't any commodity (like gold) backing them -- instead, they are merely willed into existence by the government (fiat is latin for "let it be done"). In particular, the Federal Reserve (which is a Central Bank created by Congress) is the part of the government (essentially functions as an independent branch, alongside the executive, legislative, and judicial, though there is some congressional oversight and presidential influence) that is responsible for willing the money into existence -- but they still need to get it into circulation somehow, and they can't just give it to some people. So here's what they've devised: first, the Federal Reserve sells treasury securities (in auctions), so they get a bunch of dollars. Next, the federal reserve buys up securities at a higher price than they sold them -- thus injecting the money that they've just willed into existence into the market (by giving it to people who sold their treasury securities). So you can imagine that if the US defaults in its debt payments, then people lose confidence and stop buying treasury securities, which means the Fed can't inject any more money into the market, which means the Fed can't cause inflation, and without inflation, investors don't have incentive to spend (cause inflation means the value of their money is decreasing, so keeping a positive inflation rate means investors keep investing), and if investors don't invest, then companies stop having money to pay people, and we find ourselves in a recession!

there's two pieces glossed over near the end here -- first, a lot of US treasury securities are owned by foreign countries like Japan and China and the UK. Essentially, the foreign countries are making a bet that the US government is going to remain stable and able to , and also ensuring that if necessary, they could sell those treasury securities back to the Federal Reserve if they ever needed dollars -- that's just another negative effect if people lose confidence in US treasury securities (because the US government defaults on its debt), those foreign countries probably aren't gonna buy treasury securities, which makes it harder for people in the US to trade with them. The second point is "the federal reserve interest rate", which you've almost certainly heard about -- the federal reserve is a central bank, and like any other central bank, it also gives out loans -- those loans are mostly taken out by other banks, which themselves give loans to other people. Those loans have an interest rate attached -- your mortgage interest rate is probably higher than the fed's interest rate, but your bank might have taken a loan from a regional branch of the fed, which might have in turn taken the loan from the main fed. By reducing interest rates, the fed can make it easier to borrow money, and by encouraging borrowing, they encourage investors to spend more. On the other hand, by increasing interest rates, they can take money out of the market, and use that to curb inflation. Currently, we're in the middle of a recession (due to the pandemic), so we want investors to spend more, so the fed's interest rates have been near 0% for almost a year now, but the Fed projects that they're going to increase  it sometime next year.
