title: Ideology

Historically, philosophers have viewed ideologies in a lot of different ways -- Terry Eagleton lists at least 16
Marx's view was particularly influential. He thought that ideology was part of the superstructure of society (recall that "base" refers to the relations of production, "superstructure" refers to everything else, the laws, the culture, religion, politics, etc.), and that the base greatly influenced the superstructure, to the point where the superstructure was almost fully determined by the ruling class interests. ideology, then, is a system of falsehoods promoted by the ruling class, as part of the superstructure, in order to sustain their position, like a veil that prevented the working class from developing class consciousness. for example, religious ideology functions to justify the capitalist hierarchy (like in Andrew Carnegie's Gospel of Wealth, which argues that wealthier people ought to have their money because it was given to them by god, and they know how to use it best, or in the Puritanical idea that you should suffer and work hard through life, even if the world is unjust, because you'll be ultimately rewarded when you go to heaven). similarly, Marx says that liberal ideology, with ideas like political representative democracy, universal rights, and equality under the law, also functions to justify the capitalist hierarchy, because it superficially presents a world with that equality, where the working class ostensibly has the freedom it purports to grant, while in reality, the political system is largely controlled by the capitalist class and that equal rights are meaningless if certain people have a lot more freedom to actually exercise those rights than others (for example, you can't really use your right to protest if you're working 12 hours a day in a mine in a company town somewhere)
so that's marx's view of ideology -- a set of ideas which serve to prop and and reproduce the existing set of social relations
engel's refers to that view of ideology as a "false consciousness" which is a term you might hear thrown around
it just stands in contrast to "class consciousness" -- the proletariat is either class conscious, aware of their circumstances in the capitalist mode of production and actively fighting to further their goals, or is functioning under a "false conscious", functioning under some ideology of the ruling class, furthering goals that do not benefit the proletariat
