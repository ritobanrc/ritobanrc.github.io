_God of Small Things_ is a brilliant work by Indian author Arundhati Roy. It's extremely well written, discusses issues between Marxism, Communism, Casteism, and Classism, and how they interplay with the Love Laws. Roy has an extremely thorough understanding of Marxism, analyzing it very critically. A couple key points: 

- She discusses how Marxism is similar to a religion. It presents a convenient dichotomy between the sinners and the "elect", tells the "elect" that they will eventually win in some "rapturous" event, and in the mean time, satiates them with many orthodox texts. This isn't to say that Roy dislikes Marxism -- she is almost certainly a Marxist herself, but it's a really great elucidation of how tankies come about.
- She discusses how the CPI doesn't defend Velutha after he and Ammu violate the love laws -- CPI opposes class, but other issues are your own ballpark. Really great repudiation of class reductionism.
- She critiques "feel good" Marxists, people like Chacko who are only party members until it begins to hurt themselves (but also makes us empathize with them, forcing us to understand that they are not just scum that need to be purged).
    - "Just a case of a spoiled princeling playing Comrade! Comrade! An oxford avatar of the old zamindar mentality--a landlord forcing his attentions on women who depended on him for their livlihood."
- Really great criticism of the police -- how they represent the deepest, subconscious beliefs of the community, about how the world should be organized, that Untouchables ought to stay Untouchables
    - There's also some really well done discussion of this about how the other Touchable factory workers despise Velutha for his privelaged station in the family despite being Untouchable. 


# some quotes that I decided to save for soem reason

"It is curious how sometimes the memory of death lives on for so much longer than the memory of the life that it purloined." -- Not much to say here. It's referring to Sophie, but I think it could be applied to Velutha. 

"That Big God howled like a hot wind, and demanded Obeisance. Then Small God (cozy and contained, private and limited) came away cauterized, laughing numbly at his own temerity" -- there's a great discussion of how small things can be extremely meaningful in this book. I suppose this is paralleled in the overall political message as well, that small things like direct action and unionizing and letting a Paravan into your house are as important as "the revolution". 

"though she said that choosing between her husband's name and her father's name didn't give a woman much of a choice" -- a small thing, but a very important thing, nonetheless, especially when woman are oppressed. I also love the move not to give Ammu's ex-husband a name. He's a misogynistic ass that tried to prostitute her out to his boss.

"Kochu Maria wiped the blob away with her thumb. The juice left a red smear. She put the money into her bodice. Meat-smelling blood money". Cause the money was earned off the sweat of others. 

Overall, the writing is really nice. The descriptions are reasonably vivid, with a good mix of flowery descriptions and terse ones. I quite like the simplistic language and worldview of Estha and Rahel, contrasted with that of older people, or themselves later on. The non-linear timeline also works really well, and makes some scenes that much more impactful. Sophie Mol's funeral at the beginning was really well written, as was the scene when Estha is being Returned, and has to say goodbye to Ammu (near the end).

I feel like this book had _a lot_ of symbolism that I didn't pick up on. Every so often, I would notice something, like the rain indicating a rebirth of Esta and Rahel, that a storm was coming, or that Estha's puff and Rahel's Love-in-Tokyo represented their "bourgeouis aspects", or how Velya Paappen (Velutha's father) is likened to Abraham, except ironically, cause Velutha's father is happy to sacrifice his son to set it right, while Abraham is loth to, or when Estha is in the bathroom and then he cleans stuff, how that deals with classism and untouchability. 

Anyway, this was pretty unstructured. Overall, it's quite a good story with some great discussion of history and socialist ideas.
